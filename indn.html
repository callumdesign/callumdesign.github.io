Micro Charge on the go

This project was completed in cooperation with Micro Mobility and Victoria University of Wellington. The purpose of the project was to design & complimentary charging service for Micro customers. The service aims to provide Micro customers with an appealing and convenient method of charging electric scooters in order to encourage their widespread use. By doing so, Micro Charge on the Go builds on Micro Mobility's "world first" branding value to reinforce the idea that electric scooters are a useful and convenient urban transport option.

With the ability to leave your scooter unattended as it charges, it is important to ensure that it is securely locked up until the owner is ready to retrieve it. For this reason, the bench has been designed to use a locking system to keep the scooter safely contained for a set duration of time. The system is comprised of several locking doorways which each lead to a cavity beneath the bench in which the scooter can be securely housed.

Since it is possible for many different people to come and go while using the recharge station, the method for setting the lock would be customisable for each person. For this reason, a conceptual app was theorised which connects a digital locking system via wifi or bluetooth to the user's smart phone. Such a system may also be used to alert the user when their scooter is fully charged or when other users are waiting to use the recharge station. Since the scooter may be left for a long period of time during charging, each scooter compartment would need to be weatherproof and unable to be tampered with, to keep the scooter safe and secure. Along with the smartphone locking method, each compartment would be protected against vandalism or theft by using a double thickness steel doorway.

The cradle - binaural audio prototype.

This project was completed in cooperation with Jacob Marsh, Caela Heke and The Comfort Group and Victoria University of Wellington. The purpose of the project was to create a tangible interaction around sleeping. The concept was based around the effects of binaural audio stimulation and its efficacy for encouraging relaxation. The concept was based on studies sourced from The Journal of Physiology & Behavior (Volume 63, Issue 2, January 1998, Pages 249–252), Faculty of Mechanical Engineering and Robotics University of Science and Technology of Krakow Poland, and Music, science, and the rhythmic brain : cultural and clinical implications by Jonathan Berger and Gabe Turow.

As a group we developed a wall mounted speaker system which provided a range of binaural frequencies to encourage a restful and relaxed state before sleeping. The Cradle projects audio in a sorround sound fashion in order to create stereo sound which is crucial in ensuring that the binaural effect is achieved. Our prototype was 3D printed, however production models could be CNC routed from wood to offer a range of luxurious finishes. We also used Arduino in order to create a working prototype. The binaural effect was triggered by a touch sensor mounted beneath the fabric surface.

Quickfold Chair

The aim of this project has been to create a chair which embodies simplicity and pragmatic design philosophy. Initial influence for the chair came from English designer Jasper Morrison’s plywood chair and his simplistic vision towards design. The chair speaks about the most reduced, yet functional solution to public seating. The chair is formed from 1.5mm galvanised steel sheet, which provides a robust and durable surface that can be left outside and is resistant to the wear and tear which public furniture is subjected to.

Each of the right-angled folds in the material add stiffness and integrity to the structure, allowing the fine 1.5mm sheet to support bodyweight. The manufacturing technique for the chair includes waterjet cutting and brake pressing. The process begins with a standard sized sheet of galvanised steel, measuring 2,438mm x 1,219mm. A waterjet cutter must be used due to the folding tabs which would otherwise result in inaccurate cuts if machined with hand tools. Laser cutting is not appropriate for use with galvanised materials as the zinc layer can produce toxic fumes and flammable hydrogen gas as a by-product of the cutting process. When the outlines have been cut, the prepared sheet is pressed into the final form with a hydraulic brake press or hand operated press. The front leg section of the chair folds inwards towards the centre, providing a stable base of support.

The supporting members each have a 10mm right angled fold to provide further resistance to bowing and flexion under load. To further prevent the legs from bowing, they meet the bottom of the chair and are situated within a narrow channel cut into the galvanised sheet. The backrest section of the chair provides support through the same stiffening techniques, with these folded members forming a junction with the backrest. Each fold extends inwards 70mm, providing ample support for flexion of the material. Both the front legs and backrest sections are equipped with supporting tabs, which fold inward at right angles to further stiffen and increase support for members under compression when a load is applied to the chair. Final measurements of the chair after folding include 380mm leg height, 360mm depth and 360mm back support resulting in a total height of 740mm and width of 450mm.
